## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The March 11, 2026 Portland City Council meeting was a packed 4-hour session that began with a ceremonial recognition of DJ OG One Day before diving into substantive policy matters. The meeting addressed several significant items including environmental code streamlining, campaign finance reforms, LGBTQ+ civil rights protections, and major funding decisions around the Portland Clean Energy Community Benefits Fund.

A notable theme throughout the meeting was tension around process and scheduling, with Council President pushing to move through a heavy 17-item agenda while some councilors expressed frustration about items being indefinitely postponed from previous meetings. The most contentious discussions centered on campaign finance code amendments, where councilors debated the balance between preventing weaponization of complaints and ensuring due process protections.

The meeting concluded with significant debate over the Cully Tax Increment Financing District action plan, where concerns about administrative overhead costs dominated discussion. Several councilors, particularly Candace Avalos (District 1) and Jamie Dunphy (District 1), raised pointed questions about nearly one-third of TIF funds going toward administrative costs rather than direct community benefits.

Two agenda items were ultimately postponed to March 18th due to time constraints, highlighting ongoing challenges with agenda management and the breadth of issues before the new council structure.

## TOPIC FLAGS

### **Real Estate & Housing**

**Portland Clean Energy Fund Housing Programs** [02:10:24]
Councilors debated removing workforce development standards from housing programs funded through the Clean Energy Fund. Sameer Kanal (District 2) questioned whether workforce standards were being eliminated across all PCF programs or just housing development [02:20:26]. The changes were made in response to a disparity study, though completion timeline remains unclear.

**Cully TIF District Housing Programs** [03:28:00]
The 5-year action plan includes 13 permanent affordability units and approximately 20 down payment assistance loans. Elana Pirtle-Guiney (District 2) expressed hope for expanding homeownership opportunities for Cully residents. The down payment assistance loans are structured as no-interest, no-payment loans that begin forgiveness at year 15.

*Development Implications*: Changes to workforce standards in PCF housing programs could affect developer requirements and project economics. The Cully TIF district represents a community-led development model that other districts may replicate.

**Cully Tax Increment Financing District** [03:01:40]
Major debate over the district's 5-year action plan, with significant concern about administrative overhead. *Development Implications*: High administrative costs in TIF districts could affect project viability and community benefits. The appointment signals continued focus on development partnership agility.
