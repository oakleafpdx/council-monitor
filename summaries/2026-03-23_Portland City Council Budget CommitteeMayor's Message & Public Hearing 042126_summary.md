## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Portland City Council convened as the Budget Committee to receive Mayor Keith Wilson's proposed FY 2026-27 budget and hear public testimony. The meeting began with a procedural transition from City Council to Budget Committee, with all councilors present except Tiffany Koyama Lane (District 3), who arrived later due to a pre-scheduled event.

CFO Jonas Biery (CFO / Deputy City Administrator for Budget & Finance) presented the budget overview, describing it as "the most difficult task I've ever experienced" in his 25 years in public sector [00:11:26]. The proposed budget totals $8.5 billion, representing a 0.5% decrease from the previous year, with the general fund facing a $160 million shortfall. Biery emphasized the "severely constrained resources and extremely high anxiety" surrounding the budget process, while thanking City Budget Director Ruth Levine and the entire budget team for their extraordinary efforts.

Mayor Keith Wilson delivered his budget message, framing the fiscal challenges within Portland's broader recovery narrative. He referenced Mayor Vera Katz's statue as a symbol of Portland's enduring promise to remain "vibrant, safe, and prosperous" [00:16:51]. The mayor outlined four cornerstones of his budget: shared vision, shared priorities, shared necessity, and shared sacrifices, while emphasizing that the budget "keeps every fire station open," "funds every park and community center," and "keeps every Portland police officer and investigator on the job" [00:21:59].

The meeting featured extensive public testimony from 34 speakers, with 2-minute time limits. The testimony was dominated by city employees and union representatives, particularly from AFSCME Local 189, expressing concerns about proposed layoffs and cuts to core services. The overall tone was one of fiscal urgency tempered by calls to preserve essential services and protect city workers.

## 3. TOPIC FLAGS

### City Budget & Finance
The meeting centered entirely on the proposed FY 2026-27 budget, revealing a severe fiscal crisis. Jonas Biery (CFO / Deputy City Administrator for Budget & Finance) reported an $8.5 billion total budget with a $160 million general fund shortfall [00:11:26]. The general fund discretionary budget decreased from $804 million to $743 million (8% reduction). The budget relies heavily on one-time funding sources, including Portland Clean Energy Fund (PCF) interest transfers, reserve drawdowns, and fee increases. Several councilors, including Sameer Kanal (District 2) and Angelita Morillo (District 3), questioned the sustainability of using PCF funds for non-climate programs [02:02:34, 02:13:49].

### Public Safety
Mayor Wilson emphasized preserving core public safety functions, stating the budget "keeps every Portland police officer and investigator on the job" [00:26:37]. However, significant cuts were proposed: Portland Police Bureau faces $17.6 million in reductions through external services cuts (35%), technology/fleet reductions (20%), and elimination of 80% of Public Safety Support Specialist positions. Portland Fire and Rescue will eliminate one engine at a station with two engines and reduce three medical rescue units from 24-hour to peak-hour operations [00:26:37]. Councilor Green questioned whether non-sworn officer cuts were informed by the ongoing call allocation study [02:12:13].

### Homelessness & Shelter Policy
The budget proposes ending fund exchanges with Multnomah County for homeless services, with the city focusing on outreach and core shelter services. City shelter services face a net 31% reduction to $39.9 million, while outreach services are cut 37% to $2.8 million [00:26:37]. Multiple public testifiers, including Tavaris Means (former AFSCME Local 1790 member) and Misha Litvak (AFSCME Local 1790 president), criticized these cuts as counterproductive, arguing they would increase street homelessness and strain other city services [00:57:30, 01:04:17].

### Climate & Sustainability Policy
Significant controversy emerged over proposed use of Portland Clean Energy Fund (PCF) dollars. The Public Environment Management Office (PMO) would see a 68% general fund reduction, partially offset by PCF realignments [00:26:37]. Multiple speakers, including Noelle Studer-Spivak from 350 PDX and Emily Stebbins, strongly opposed diverting PCF funds from climate programs [01:37:36, 01:35:50]. The Urban Forestry Program faces further cuts despite community testimony about the critical need for tree preservation and canopy equity work.

### Infrastructure & Transportation
The budget assumes passage of a transportation utility fee and street damage restoration fee to maintain transportation infrastructure [00:29:15]. PBOT faces significant technical support cuts, with Nick LaRue testifying that eliminating GIS and technical positions would impact critical systems including street sweeping tracking, asset management, and Clean Water Act compliance [00:45:48]. The 311 system will see reduced hours and 4 FTE reductions.

### System Development Charges (SDCs) & Permitting Fees
Portland Permitting and Development will raise fees by $3.2 million to cover existing service levels, while the tree permitting program faces a $2.9 million reduction [00:28:52]. This represents continued pressure on development-related fees to fill budget gaps. Amanda Liston, representing tree permitting staff, warned that further cuts would increase permit processing delays and reduce public safety oversight of hazardous trees [00:37:07].

### Real Estate & Housing
Housing-related programs face mixed impacts. Portland Solutions reduces some services despite realigning county pass-through funds. The Portland Housing Bureau maintains most services but takes cuts to external materials and legal services totaling $675,000 [00:28:52]. Prosper Portland expands grants by $1 million while cutting workforce development (15%) and small business support (20%).
