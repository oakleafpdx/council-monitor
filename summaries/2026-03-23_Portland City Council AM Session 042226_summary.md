## 3. TOPIC FLAGS

### Infrastructure & Transportation

The Transportation Utility Fee dominated the session, with extensive discussion of Portland's critical infrastructure needs. Mayor Keith Wilson [01:30:17] described it as a "legacy ordinance" emphasizing the $6.6 billion maintenance backlog, with 64% of busy streets and 72% of local streets in poor condition. 

Key supporters included Oregon Walks, Verde, Families for Safe Streets, and PROTEC17 union representatives who emphasized the connection between deferred maintenance and public safety. Opposition testimony focused on the regressive nature of utility-based taxation and preference for voter-approved measures.

The ordinance includes amendments dedicating 25% of revenues to Vision Zero and Sidewalk Improvement Program (SIP), addressing concerns from pedestrian and bicycle advocates. Councilor Loretta Smith [01:26:30] highlighted this as crucial for District 1 and District 4 sidewalk needs.

**Implications:** This represents the most significant local transportation funding initiative in Portland's recent history, with potential major impacts on development costs and infrastructure capacity.

### City Budget & Finance

Multiple references to Portland's broader fiscal challenges emerged during TUF discussion. Councilor Dan Ryan [01:31:16] noted the fee comes alongside proposed 6.6% water/sewer rate increases, creating cumulative affordability pressures.

Councilor Sameer Kanal [01:40:00] emphasized "generational equity" concerns, describing inheriting a city from leaders "who did not plan for the future." The discussion revealed ongoing tension between infrastructure investment needs and affordability concerns.

**Implications:** Suggests continued pressure on development-related fees and potential impacts on housing affordability through utility cost pass-throughs.

### Real Estate & Housing

Limited direct housing discussion occurred, though multifamily housing concerns emerged in testimony. Jonathan Clay representing Multifamily Northwest [01:23:02] opposed the per-unit fee structure for multifamily properties, arguing it creates inequitable burden on housing providers rather than residents.

The Historic Landmarks Commission discussion touched briefly on development capacity concerns, with Councilor Mitch Green [00:30:39] questioning trade-offs between historic preservation and housing production capacity.

**Implications:** TUF implementation could increase operating costs for multifamily properties, while historic preservation processes continue to face scrutiny regarding development efficiency.
