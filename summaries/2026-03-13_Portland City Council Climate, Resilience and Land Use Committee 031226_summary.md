## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The final meeting of Portland's Climate, Resilience and Land Use Committee addressed three key agenda items: a Historic Landmarks Commission reappointment, an urgent resolution to accelerate Inner East Side rezoning for housing production, and a comprehensive update on Multnomah County's climate and energy trends. The committee, co-chaired by Angelita Morillo (District 3) and Steve Novick (District 3), demonstrated strong momentum around pro-housing policies while wrapping up the committee's work before Portland's transition to a new committee structure.

The meeting's centerpiece was the "Inner East Side for All" resolution, co-sponsored by Angelita Morillo (District 3), Mitch Green (District 4), and Candace Avalos (District 1). This resolution directs the city administrator to develop an accelerated timeline for upzoning the Inner East Side, with a goal of bringing zoning changes to council by June 30, 2027 [25:34]. The initiative could potentially enable up to 143,000 additional housing units in transit-rich, high-opportunity neighborhoods that are currently zoned for low density.

Strong community support was evident through testimony from Portland Neighbors Welcome, Verde, local developers, and construction workers, emphasizing the urgent need to address Portland's housing crisis through zoning reform. The committee heard that Portland needs approximately 6,000 new housing units annually but is currently building at roughly half that rate [59:38]. The resolution passed 4-1 after accepting an amendment from Steve Novick (District 3) to acknowledge potential barriers to the June 2027 timeline [33:08].

The meeting concluded with a comprehensive climate presentation showing Multnomah County carbon emissions have declined 26% since 1990 while population grew 38% [41:44]. However, the data revealed concerning trends in diesel fuel consumption, up 70% compared to gasoline's 20% decline [50:48], highlighting challenges in transportation sector decarbonization.

## TOPIC FLAGS

### Real Estate & Housing

The Inner East Side for All initiative represents Portland's most significant housing policy development in this meeting. Mitch Green (District 4) emphasized that "growth is a function of land use" and argued for pursuing "legally required minimum process" rather than typical bureaucratic timelines [29:01]. The resolution addresses Portland's housing emergency, declared over a decade ago, with the city falling short of its 6,000 annual unit production target.

**Key Development Impacts**: 
- Current Inner East Side capacity: ~22,000 units under existing zoning
- Proposed capacity: 143,000+ units with conceptual upzoning [45:13]
- Focus on "4 floors and corner stores" development pattern
- Elimination of artificial constraints like FAR limits and step-down heights advocated by developer Curtis Reichstadt [47:15]

**Next Steps**: 90-day report due to council with concrete implementation plan, geographic scope, and infrastructure coordination requirements.

### Land Use & Zoning

Extensive discussion centered on accelerating zoning reform processes. Steve Novick (District 3) questioned whether Planning Commission review could be streamlined, though Bureau of Planning and Sustainability staff emphasized the importance of meaningful public engagement [08:14]. The resolution specifically targets Inner East Side neighborhoods where two-thirds remain zoned for low density despite transit access and urban amenities [41:52].

**Regulatory Considerations**:
- Discussion of eliminating "discussion draft" phase and moving directly to "proposed draft"
- Coordination required with infrastructure bureaus (PBOT, BES, Water)
- Anti-displacement analysis methodology referenced from Residential Infill Project
- Legal defensibility under Oregon land use law emphasized

### System Development Charges (SDCs) & Permitting Fees

Sameer Kanal (District 2) specifically inquired whether the 90-day analysis would include SDC impact assessment [23:50]. Director Ting Lu from Portland Public Utilities confirmed that infrastructure cost projections and resource needs would be defined as part of the accelerated timeline development [24:28]. Angelita Morillo (District 3) noted that SDC impacts are already being tracked through Housing Committee amendments.

### Infrastructure & Transportation

Transportation planning emerged as a critical coordination element. The Bureau of Planning and Sustainability completed an Inner East Side Infrastructure Assessment that stress-tested utility systems under high-density scenarios. Kyle Diesner noted that Portland is developing new transportation inventory methods using VMT data rather than fuel sales, which will provide better insight for land use decisions [50:48].

**Transit Considerations**: Discussion addressed TriMet service challenges, with some bus lines experiencing frequency cuts rather than improvements. Long-term zoning decisions must balance current transit capacity with density's potential to support enhanced service.

### Climate & Sustainability Policy

Kyle Diesner's presentation revealed mixed climate progress. While overall emissions declined 26% since 1990, transportation sector emissions remained "incredibly stubborn" until the pandemic [41:44]. Concerning trends include:
- Diesel fuel consumption up 70% vs. gasoline down 20% [50:48]
- Natural gas consumption up 18% overall, with residential/commercial usage up 62% and 43% respectively [48:02]
- Grid electricity renewable content at 33% but expected to improve with state mandates by 2030

**Policy Implications**: Portland's Renewable Fuel Standard phases out fossil diesel by 2030. Consumption-based emissions (from imported goods) are nearly double local emissions, highlighting broader sustainability challenges [56:26].

### City Budget & Finance

Mitch Green (District 4) framed the housing crisis within Portland's fiscal context, noting the city faces a $170 million budget gap driven by regional competition and limited capacity for direct development subsidies [29:01]. The zoning reform approach represents a "low or no-cost" strategy for enabling housing production and generating additional property tax revenue through lot redevelopment and reassessment.
